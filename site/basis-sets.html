Level 1 · Language of orbitals

What is a basis set?

Every molecular orbital is written as a finite expansion in atom-centered basis functions:

\[ \psi_i(\mathbf r)=\sum_{\mu=1}^{K} C_{\mu i}\,\chi_\mu(\mathbf r). \]

The basis set chooses the finite subspace. A better basis gives orbitals more ways to move, polarize, stretch, and correlate — but every extra function increases cost and can make the overlap matrix harder to invert.

🧩

Basis function

A local shape: s, p, d, f, tight core, diffuse tail, auxiliary fit, etc.

🎚️

Coefficient

The MO recipe: coefficients mix local shapes into bonding, antibonding, lone-pair, Rydberg, and diffuse states.

⚖️

Tradeoff

More flexibility lowers variational error but increases cost, BSSE risk, and linear-dependence risk.

Level 2 · Playable plot

Gaussian shape lab: sculpt a basis function

Most molecular programs use Gaussian-type orbitals because Gaussian products make multicenter integrals tractable:

\[ g_{\ell m}(\mathbf r-\mathbf A)=N\,r_A^\ell Y_{\ell m}(\widehat{\mathbf r_A}) e^{-\alpha r_A^2}. \]

Level 3 · AO arena

See how s, p, d, and f functions look

Angular momentum is not decoration: it is how density bends into bonds, lone pairs, backbonding, and correlation holes.

Level 4 · Contraction forge

Forge primitives into a contracted basis

A practical basis function is often a contraction:

\[ \chi_\mu(\mathbf r)=\sum_p d_{p\mu} g_p(\mathbf r). \]

Level 5 · Matrix dungeon

Basis sets become matrices

Because basis functions overlap, the orbital problem becomes a generalized eigenvalue problem:

\[ \mathbf F\mathbf C=\mathbf S\mathbf C\boldsymbol\varepsilon, \quad S_{\mu\nu}=\langle\chi_\mu|\chi_\nu\rangle. \]

Level 8 · BSSE duel

Fight basis-set superposition error

In a dimer, monomer A can borrow monomer B's functions. That extra flexibility artificially stabilizes the complex.

Level 9 · Linear-dependence alarm

Too many diffuse functions can break the game

Diffuse functions are essential for tails, but if two functions become nearly identical, the smallest eigenvalue of \(S\) collapses.

Level 10 · CBS extrapolation lab

Estimate the complete-basis-set limit

Correlation-consistent ladders let you fit convergence patterns, often using inverse powers of the cardinal number \(X\):

\[ E_X \approx E_\mathrm{CBS}+A X^{-3}. \]

Level 11 · Cost boss

Advanced level · Integral engine

Where basis sets become the Hamiltonian

At the advanced level, a basis set is not only a list of shapes. It is an instruction set for building every matrix element in quantum chemistry. Once you choose exponents, centers, angular momenta, and contractions, the computer must evaluate millions to trillions of integrals:

\[ S_{\mu\nu}=\langle \chi_\mu|\chi_\nu\rangle,\quad T_{\mu\nu}=\left\langle \chi_\mu\left|-\tfrac12\nabla^2\right|\chi_\nu\right\rangle,\quad V_{\mu\nu}=\left\langle \chi_\mu\left|-\sum_A\frac{Z_A}{r_A}\right|\chi_\nu\right\rangle, \]
\[ (\mu\nu|\lambda\sigma)=\iint \frac{\chi_\mu(\mathbf r_1)\chi_\nu(\mathbf r_1)\chi_\lambda(\mathbf r_2)\chi_\sigma(\mathbf r_2)}{r_{12}}\,d\mathbf r_1\,d\mathbf r_2. \]
🧮

Integral type

Overlap \(S\) controls conditioning; kinetic \(T\) punishes tight curvature; nuclear attraction \(V\) pulls density to nuclei; ERIs build Coulomb and exchange.

ζ

Basis knobs

Exponents, contraction length, angular momentum, diffuse tails, and shell layout decide which integrals are large, tiny, numerous, or unstable.

⚙️

Algorithms

Production codes use Gaussian product theorem, Boys functions, recurrences, quadrature, symmetry, Schwarz screening, and derivative recurrences.

Level 14 · Gaussian product theorem game

The trick that makes Gaussian basis sets computationally powerful

Two primitive Gaussians on different centers multiply into one new Gaussian on a product center \(\mathbf P\), times a distance penalty. This is why Gaussian integrals are tractable while Slater-type tails are chemically nicer but computationally harder.

\[ e^{-\alpha|\mathbf r-\mathbf A|^2}e^{-\beta|\mathbf r-\mathbf B|^2} = e^{-\mu |\mathbf A-\mathbf B|^2}e^{-p|\mathbf r-\mathbf P|^2},\quad p=\alpha+\beta,\;\mu=\frac{\alpha\beta}{p},\;\mathbf P=\frac{\alpha\mathbf A+\beta\mathbf B}{p}. \]

Level 15 · One-electron integral lab

Overlap, kinetic energy, and nuclear attraction are basis-set measurements

For two normalized s-type Gaussian primitives, the overlap and kinetic integrals already show the deep basis connection: diffuse functions overlap far away; tight functions have large curvature and therefore large kinetic energy; nuclear attraction depends on where the product center lands relative to the nucleus.

Level 16 · Boys function cavern

The Coulomb singularity becomes a Boys function

Nuclear attraction and electron-repulsion integrals over Gaussians reduce to special functions after Gaussian products are formed. The central object is the Boys function:

\[ F_m(T)=\int_0^1 u^{2m}e^{-Tu^2}\,du. \]

Level 17 · Four-index ERI tensor raid

The expensive object: \((\mu\nu|\lambda\sigma)\)

Electron-repulsion integrals couple two basis-function products. If you have \(K\) basis functions, the naive count scales like \(K^4\). Real codes survive by using shell structure, permutational symmetry, contraction strategies, density fitting, locality, and Schwarz screening.

Level 18 · Algorithm and derivative boss

Recurrences, quadrature, screening, and gradients

Advanced integral engines do not evaluate every Cartesian polynomial from scratch. They climb recurrence ladders, transform Cartesian/spherical components, screen shell quartets, and differentiate integrals analytically for forces, Hessians, response, and basis optimization.

Final rule: basis-set choice is also integral-engine choice: choose the smallest basis that is balanced for the property and computationally stable for the integral problem. Geometry may tolerate double-ζ/polarized; response properties, anions, Rydberg states, weak binding, and correlated benchmarks usually require diffuse functions, higher ζ, and/or systematic extrapolation.
