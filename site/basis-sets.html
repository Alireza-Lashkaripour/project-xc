Seven levels of basis-set thinking

Level 1 / 7

Level 1

What is a basis?

In molecular electronic-structure theory, every molecular orbital is expanded in atom-centered functions:

\[ \psi_i(\mathbf r)=\sum_{\mu=1}^{K} C_{\mu i}\,\chi_\mu(\mathbf r). \]

The exact orbital lives in an infinite-dimensional function space. The basis set chooses a finite subspace. Increasing the basis usually lowers the variational energy, but it also increases cost and can create numerical problems.

Basis function \(\chi_\mu\)

A local atom-centered shape: s, p, d, f, diffuse tail, tight core, etc.

Coefficient \(C_{\mu i}\)

The MO recipe: how much of every local shape appears in orbital \(i\).

Basis size \(K\)

The number of functions; larger \(K\) gives more flexibility and higher computational cost.

Level 2 · Mini-game

Shape lab: build a radial Gaussian basis function

Most molecular programs use Gaussian-type orbitals because Gaussian products make multicenter integrals tractable. A primitive Gaussian is roughly

\[ g_{\ell m}(\mathbf r-\mathbf A)=N\,r_A^\ell Y_{\ell m}(\widehat{\mathbf r_A}) e^{-\alpha r_A^2}. \]

Level 3

Primitive, contracted, split-valence, polarization, diffuse

A practical basis function is often a contraction — a fixed linear combination of primitives:

\[ \chi_\mu(\mathbf r)=\sum_p d_{p\mu} g_p(\mathbf r). \]

Minimal

One basis function per occupied atomic orbital. Fast, but chemically cramped.

Split-valence

Two or more radial valence functions, e.g. double-ζ or triple-ζ flexibility.

Polarized/diffuse

Angular flexibility and long-range density: essential for bonding distortions, anions, Rydberg states, response properties.

Level 4

How basis sets enter HF/DFT calculations

After choosing \(\{\chi_\mu\}\), the continuum Schrödinger/Kohn–Sham problem becomes matrix algebra. Because atom-centered functions overlap, the eigenproblem is generalized:

\[ \mathbf F\mathbf C=\mathbf S\mathbf C\boldsymbol\varepsilon, \quad S_{\mu\nu}=\langle\chi_\mu|\chi_\nu\rangle. \]
basis functions
→
overlap \(S\), kinetic \(T\), nuclear \(V\)
→
two-electron integrals / grids
→
SCF orbitals and density

At PhD level, basis choice is not decoration: it controls variational flexibility, basis-set superposition error, integral conditioning, density response, and extrapolation to the complete-basis-set limit.

Level 6

Pathologies: how basis sets fail

Linear dependence

Two functions become almost the same; \(S\) has tiny eigenvalues, SCF becomes unstable, and diffuse-heavy sets can fail.

BSSE

In dimers, monomer A borrows basis functions from monomer B, artificially stabilizing the complex. Use counterpoise or larger balanced bases.

Wrong tail

No diffuse functions means bad electron affinities, Rydberg excitations, dipoles/polarizabilities, and weak interactions.

Wrong angular flexibility

No polarization functions means bonds cannot bend electron density into chemically realistic shapes.

Level 7 · Decision game

Choose the basis set strategy

Select a scenario and a basis strategy. The score explains what the basis is missing.

Practical rule: choose the smallest basis that is balanced for the property. Geometry may tolerate double-ζ/polarized; response properties, anions, Rydberg states, weak binding, and correlated benchmarks usually require diffuse, higher-ζ, and/or systematic extrapolation.
