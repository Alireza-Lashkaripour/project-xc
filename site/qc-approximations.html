Approximation-model boundary

Exact means exact only inside the stated finite or analytic model. Variational bounds require a normalized admissible trial and the right Hamiltonian hypotheses. Perturbative ratios and overlap thresholds are diagnostics, not universal theorems. Signed computational errors can cancel and are not statistical uncertainties. Every laboratory names what was solved, what was omitted, and what evidence would be needed next.

Level 1 · Classification before calculation

Every approximation needs a passport

A computed number can be algebraically exact and scientifically approximate at the same time. First identify the operation; then identify the layer it omits. Those are different questions.

Level 2 · Remove accidental units

Dimensionless scaling forge

For $H=p^2/(2m)+kx^2/2+\beta x^4$, choose $\omega=\sqrt{k/m}$ and $\ell=\sqrt{\hbar/(m\omega)}$. The scaled shape is controlled by $g=\beta\hbar/(m^2\omega^3)$—not by three raw parameters independently.

Scale the quartic oscillator

  1. Change a physical scale.
  2. Watch $\omega$, $\ell$, and $\hbar\omega$ move.
  3. Track the single dimensionless shape coupling $g$.
  4. State what the potential plot does not solve.
Professor mode · Why scaling comes first

Dimensionless groups reveal the actual expansion parameter, prevent unit-dependent “smallness,” and make cross-system comparisons meaningful. Scaling does not create a controlled approximation; it tells you which combinations must be controlled.

Level 4 · Restricted trial family

Gaussian variational sculptor

For the dimensionless harmonic oscillator and $\psi_\alpha\propto e^{-\alpha x^2/2}$, $T=\alpha/4$, $V=1/(4\alpha)$, and $E\ge 1/2$. Narrowing raises kinetic cost; broadening raises potential cost.

Balance the width penalties

  1. Start too diffuse.
  2. Cross the virial balance.
  3. Make the state too narrow.
  4. Use the optimizer, then explain why this exact hit is special.
Misconception: variational energies are not automatically “close.” They are upper bounds under the theorem’s hypotheses; a rigid family can still sit far above the target.

Level 5 · Finite representation

Nested-basis expedition

The same six-site tridiagonal Hamiltonian is projected into its first $n$ coordinates. Because the spaces are nested, the lowest Ritz values decrease toward the declared six-dimensional oracle.

Converge two diagnostics

  1. Choose a subspace size.
  2. Track the upper energy excess.
  3. Track the external residual.
  4. Choose a teaching tolerance and decide whether both conditions pass.
Professor mode · Why nesting matters

If two spaces are not nested, a lower energy in the second calculation does not by itself define a monotone convergence sequence. Real basis families can also change other approximations, thresholds, grids, and linear-dependence treatments at the same time.

Level 6 · The metric is part of the problem

Overlap-conditioning rescue

Nonorthogonal functions produce $Hc=ESc$. For $S=\begin{pmatrix}1&s\\s&1\end{pmatrix}$, the eigenvalues are $1\pm s$ and $\kappa_2=(1+|s|)/(1-|s|)$. Near duplication amplifies numerical sensitivity.

Approach linear dependence carefully

The warning threshold is deliberately labeled as a teaching choice. A production threshold must be justified by stability of the retained physics.

Level 7 · Isolated-state expansion

Nondegenerate perturbation ladder

For $H=\begin{pmatrix}0&\lambda V\\\lambda V&\Delta\end{pmatrix}$, compare exact diagonalization with $E^{(2)}=-(\lambda V)^2/\Delta$ and the fourth-order correction $+(\lambda V)^4/\Delta^3$.

Spend the order budget

  1. Set an unperturbed gap.
  2. Set coupling and turn on $\lambda$.
  3. Compare two retained orders.
  4. Judge the ratio without calling it a theorem.

Level 8 · Degenerate subspaces

Degenerate-subspace crossroads

Inside an exactly degenerate subspace, first-order shifts are the eigenvalues of the restricted perturbation $W$. Its diagonal entries change when the basis rotates; its trace, determinant, and eigenvalues do not.

Remove basis-dependent guessing

Misconception: “Use the diagonal correction for each original state.” That is basis dependent whenever the perturbation couples the degenerate states.

Level 9 · More terms can be worse

Asymptotic order budget

For $F(g)=\int_0^\infty e^{-t}/(1+gt)\,dt$, the formal small-$g$ expansion is $\sum_{n\ge0}(-g)^n n!$. Factorial coefficients eventually grow for every fixed $g>0$.

Find the best finite stop

  1. Choose a coupling.
  2. Advance the truncation order.
  3. Watch error fall and then rise.
  4. Compare your stop with the best displayed order.

Level 10 · Do not hide cancellation

Computational error-budget ledger

Declare a path from numerical result to representation limit to model limit to reference. The signed differences telescope exactly. Their absolute magnitudes reveal work hidden by cancellation.

Close the four-column ledger

Professor mode · Error is not uncertainty

The displayed components are deterministic signed differences along one declared path. Statistical uncertainty, parameter uncertainty, and reference uncertainty require separate definitions and combination rules. Changing the order of limits can also change the decomposition.

Level 11 · Certification needs a gap

Residual and bound inspector

In the finite spectrum $(0,1.5,4)$, build $c=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta\cos\phi,\sin\theta\sin\phi)$. The variance equals $\|(H-\mu)c\|^2$. A Temple-type lower estimate is shown only when $\mu<E_1$.

Build or break a certificate

  1. Add excited-state weight with $\theta$.
  2. Move that weight between $E_1$ and $E_2$ with $\phi$.
  3. Track Rayleigh value and residual.
  4. Cross the gap condition and watch the lower certificate disappear.
