Many-electron-model boundary

Exact means exact only in the declared finite spin-orbital space. The laboratories manipulate normalized finite determinant expansions and supplied one-/two-electron objects. They do not optimize orbitals, solve Hartree–Fock, recover a complete-basis wavefunction, add dynamic correlation, or prove full practical 2-RDM N-representability. “Structurally allowed” means an operator rank does not force a matrix element to zero; symmetry or an actual integral may still make it vanish.

Level 1 · Exchange passport

Swap coordinates; do not attach identities to electrons

For the total spin-coordinate amplitude, fermionic exchange requires \(\Psi(x_2,x_1)=-\Psi(x_1,x_2)\). A symmetric spatial factor can still occur when an antisymmetric spin factor supplies the total minus sign; this laboratory classifies the total amplitude only.

Dossiers cleared: 0 / 3

The swapped amplitudes are supplied; the exchange eigenvalue remains ungraded until commitment.
Do not say: “electron 1 moved into electron 2’s place.” Exchange compares arguments of one many-electron state; it does not track classical identity tags.

Level 2 · Occupation forge

Create a determinant—or annihilate it by occupying twice

With \(M\) ordered spin orbitals, an \(N\)-electron determinant is a bitstring with exactly \(N\) occupied entries. There are \(\binom{M}{N}\) such determinants. Repeating a creation index gives zero by Pauli antisymmetry.

Indices start at 0. Repeated or out-of-range indices are deliberate failure states.

Forges cleared: 0 / 3

Build one valid state and predict the full fixed-N determinant count.
Professor mode · Fock space versus fixed-N sector

The full fermionic Fock space over \(M\) spin orbitals has dimension \(2^M\). This chapter stays in one fixed-\(N\) sector of dimension \(\binom{M}{N}\), except when applying one creation or annihilation operator as an intermediate algebraic action.

Level 3 · Determinant sign ledger

Canonical order fixes a representation sign—not a new observable

Reordering determinant columns by a permutation \(P\) multiplies the represented determinant by \((-1)^{\mathrm{inv}(P)}\). A duplicate orbital makes the wedge product zero. Global sign remains a state convention; relative determinant phases matter in a CI expansion.

Level 4 · Determinant overlap

A determinant inherits the volume of its occupied-orbital Gram matrix

For occupied sets \(\{\chi_i\}\) and \(\{\eta_j\}\), \(\langle\Phi_\chi|\Phi_\eta\rangle=\det S\) with \(S_{ij}=\langle\chi_i|\eta_j\rangle\). For one determinant’s norm, \(S\) is its Gram matrix. Linear dependence collapses the wedge volume to zero.

Artifacts cleared: 0 / 4

The Gram entries are visible; determinant and spectrum remain withheld until commitment.
Normalization warning: the familiar \(1/\sqrt{N!}\) prefactor normalizes a determinant only when the occupied spin orbitals are orthonormal.

Level 5 · One-body Slater–Condon structure

Count substitutions before evaluating an integral

A one-body operator \(\hat O_1=\sum_{pq}o_{pq}a_p^\dagger a_q\) can connect determinants differing by zero or one orbital substitution. Two or more substitutions force the matrix element to zero. Rank allowance is necessary, not sufficient.

The differing occupations are supplied; the excitation-rank certificate is withheld.

Level 6 · Two-body structure

Direct minus exchange is an expectation value—not yet Hartree–Fock

A two-body operator can connect determinants through at most two substitutions. For one supplied determinant, its exact finite expectation contains pair terms \(J_{ij}-K_{ij}\). No orbital stationarity or self-consistency has been imposed here.

Pairs cleared: 0 / 4 · energy interpretation pending

Commit both the structural rule and the supplied-expectation interpretation.

Level 7 · Spin adaptation

Relative determinant phase chooses singlet or triplet under a declared ordering

Use canonical spin-orbital order \((a\alpha,a\beta,b\alpha,b\beta)\), \(D_1=|a\alpha\,b\beta|\), and \(D_2=|a\beta\,b\alpha|\). Then \((D_1-D_2)/\sqrt2\) is the singlet and \((D_1+D_2)/\sqrt2\) is the \(M_S=0\) triplet. A different determinant ordering changes displayed coefficient signs, not the physical spin.

Level 8 · One-particle reduced density matrix

Compress one-body information without pretending the wavefunction is recovered

For \(\gamma_{pq}=\langle a_p^\dagger a_q\rangle\), a valid spin-orbital 1-RDM is Hermitian, has \(\mathrm{Tr}\,\gamma=N\), and has natural occupations in \([0,1]\). A single determinant has idempotent \(\gamma\); a correlated multi-determinant state can have fractional occupations.

1-RDM dossiers cleared: 0 / 3

The supplied diagonal is visible. Commit before the trace/idempotency certificate is revealed.

Level 9 · Two-particle reduced density matrix

Contract pair evidence back to the one-body record

With \(\Gamma_{pq,rs}=\langle a_p^\dagger a_q^\dagger a_s a_r\rangle\), this chapter uses \(\mathrm{Tr}\,\Gamma=N(N-1)\) and \(\sum_q\Gamma_{pq,rq}=(N-1)\gamma_{pr}\). Antisymmetry, trace, and contraction are necessary checks. They are not advertised as a complete practical 2-RDM N-representability proof.

The claim file is visible; pair trace and contraction residual remain hidden.
Professor mode · what is and is not being certified

For the exact CI-generated example, the RDMs are representable because they were constructed from a normalized state. For an arbitrary candidate 2-RDM, trace, contraction, Hermiticity, and antisymmetry are only a subset of the required conditions. The broader N-representability problem is deliberately deferred.
