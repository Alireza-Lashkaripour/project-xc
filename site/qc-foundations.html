Level 1 · Change the scale

Quantum chemistry begins where classical trajectories stop being enough

A molecule contains nuclei and electrons. Classical mechanics remains useful for many nuclear motions, but an electron bound to a nucleus cannot be assigned a tiny planetary path that simultaneously explains atomic stability, discrete spectra, interference, and chemical bonding. Quantum mechanics replaces a definite microscopic path with a state and rules for predicting measurement probabilities.

10⁻¹⁰

Atomic scale

Typical bond lengths are measured in ångströms, while electronic energies are naturally compared in electronvolts or hartrees.

ℏ

Quantum action

Planck’s reduced constant sets the scale connecting wavelength, momentum, frequency, and energy.

a₀

Atomic units

Electronic-structure equations become compact when electron charge, electron mass, \(\hbar\), and the Coulomb constant are set to one.

\[ e=m_e=\hbar=4\pi\varepsilon_0=1 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \hat H_\mathrm{electronic}=-\frac12\sum_i\nabla_i^2-\sum_{iA}\frac{Z_A}{r_{iA}}+\sum_{i<j}\frac{1}{r_{ij}}+E_\mathrm{NN}. \]
Misconception warning: “quantum” does not mean that every macroscopic object visibly behaves randomly. It means the state and measurement rules are quantum; classical behavior can emerge through scale, environment, averaging, and decoherence.
Professor mode · what has already been assumed?

The displayed molecular Hamiltonian is nonrelativistic, uses the Coulomb interaction, and treats clamped nuclei in the Born–Oppenheimer electronic problem. Spin, external fields, finite nuclear mass, relativity, and quantum electrodynamic corrections require additional structure.

Level 2 · State before picture

A wavefunction is a state representation—not an electron’s drawn path

A pure quantum state is written abstractly as \(|\psi\rangle\). In the position representation, the same state is the complex amplitude \(\psi(\mathbf r)=\langle\mathbf r|\psi\rangle\). A different basis gives different coefficients for the same physical state.

\[ |\psi\rangle=\sum_k c_k|k\rangle, \qquad c_k=\langle k|\psi\rangle, \qquad \sum_k |c_k|^2=1. \]
State\(|\psi\rangle\)The basis-independent physical ray for a pure state.
Amplitude\(c_k\) or \(\psi(\mathbf r)\)A generally complex coefficient in a chosen representation.
Probability\(|c_k|^2\)The Born-rule probability for an orthonormal outcome \(|k\rangle\).
Phase\(e^{i\phi}\)Global phase is unobservable; relative phase controls interference.
Do not say: “the wavefunction is the electron cloud.” The one-electron density is related to \(|\psi|^2\), while a many-electron wavefunction depends on all electron coordinates and spin labels simultaneously.
Professor mode · rays, mixed states, and representation

Normalized vectors differing only by a global phase represent the same pure physical state. Statistical mixtures require a density operator \(\hat\rho\), not a single state vector. Later electronic-structure chapters will also distinguish many-electron wavefunctions, one-particle densities, natural orbitals, and Kohn–Sham orbitals.

Level 3 · Interactive lab 1

Probability and normalization: make the total chance equal one

The probability of detecting a particle in a region is obtained from the squared magnitude. A trial amplitude must be normalizable before it can represent a bound-state probability distribution:

\[ P(a\le x\le b)=\int_a^b |\psi(x)|^2\,dx, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\psi(x)|^2\,dx=1. \]
Misconception warning: normalization does not determine the shape. It fixes one overall magnitude after the Hamiltonian, boundary conditions, or a chosen trial model determines the form.

Level 4 · Interactive lab 2

Phase: invisible globally, decisive relatively

Multiplying the entire state by \(e^{i\phi}\) changes no probability. Placing a phase between two alternatives changes their cross term and therefore their interference:

\[ |e^{i\phi}\psi|^2=|\psi|^2, \qquad |\phi_A+e^{i\delta}\phi_B|^2=|\phi_A|^2+|\phi_B|^2+2\operatorname{Re}\!\left[e^{i\delta}\phi_A^*\phi_B\right]. \]
Professor mode · why phase still matters when orbitals can change sign

An isolated orbital’s overall sign is a convention, but relative phases among basis functions, determinants, or coupled pathways determine interference and matrix elements. Orbital rotations within an occupied or degenerate subspace can change pictures without changing the represented many-electron state or invariant observables.

Level 5 · Measurement language

Operators connect states to possible measurement outcomes

An observable is represented by a Hermitian operator. Its eigenvalues are possible outcomes. A general state need not be an eigenstate, so one measurement can yield different eigenvalues with probabilities set by the expansion coefficients.

\[ \hat A|a_k\rangle=a_k|a_k\rangle, \qquad |\psi\rangle=\sum_k c_k|a_k\rangle, \qquad P(a_k)=|c_k|^2, \qquad \langle A\rangle=\langle\psi|\hat A|\psi\rangle. \]

Professor mode · noncommuting observables

If \([\hat A,\hat B]\ne0\), the operators need not share a complete eigenbasis. This algebraic incompatibility—not merely an imperfect instrument—is the source of the standard uncertainty lower bound.

Level 6 · Dynamics and stationary states

The Schrödinger equation tells amplitudes how to evolve

\[ i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\Psi(t)\rangle=\hat H(t)|\Psi(t)\rangle. \]

For a time-independent Hamiltonian, an energy eigenstate has a spatial part \(\psi_n\) and a pure phase time factor. Its probability density is stationary even though the state carries time-dependent phase:

\[ \hat H\psi_n=E_n\psi_n, \qquad \Psi_n(\mathbf r,t)=\psi_n(\mathbf r)e^{-iE_nt/\hbar}, \qquad |\Psi_n(\mathbf r,t)|^2=|\psi_n(\mathbf r)|^2. \]
H

Hamiltonian

Encodes kinetic energy, interactions, fields, constraints, and the model boundary.

E

Stationary state

An energy eigenstate changes by phase while its probability density remains fixed.

Σ

Superposition

A mixture of different energies accumulates relative phases and can produce time-dependent observables.

“Stationary” does not mean classical stillness. It means the probability distribution and time-independent observables do not change for an energy eigenstate of a time-independent Hamiltonian.
Professor mode · time dependence and electronic structure

Ground-state quantum chemistry usually solves a stationary electronic eigenproblem under clamped nuclei. Spectroscopy, response, nonadiabatic dynamics, and explicitly driven systems require time dependence or response equations, and approximate electronic states may not be eigenstates of the exact Hamiltonian.

Level 7 · Interactive lab 3

Particle in a box: boundary conditions quantize energy

Inside an infinite one-dimensional well from \(0\) to \(L\), the wavefunction vanishes at both walls. Only standing waves with an integer number of half-wavelengths satisfy both boundaries:

\[ \psi_n(x)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \qquad E_n=\frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2mL^2}, \qquad n=1,2,3,\ldots \]
Quantization is not an arbitrary rule pasted onto classical mechanics. Here it follows from the wave equation plus boundary conditions. Different potentials produce different spectra and eigenfunctions.

Level 8 · Interactive lab 4

Uncertainty is a width trade-off, not just measurement clumsiness

For any state, the standard deviations of two observables obey the Robertson relation. Position and momentum satisfy:

\[ \sigma_A\sigma_B\ge\frac12\left|\langle[\hat A,\hat B]\rangle\right|, \qquad [\hat x,\hat p_x]=i\hbar, \qquad \sigma_x\sigma_p\ge\frac{\hbar}{2}. \]
Convention warning: width parameters, variance, standard deviation, and full width at half maximum are not interchangeable. This game defines \(\sigma_x\) as the standard deviation of \(|\psi(x)|^2\).
Professor mode · equality is special

The lower bound follows from operator algebra, but equality is not automatic. The displayed Gaussian is chosen to saturate \(\sigma_x\sigma_p=\hbar/2\). Excited, truncated, multimodal, or otherwise non-Gaussian states generally have a larger product.

Level 9 · Interactive lab 5

Spin-1/2 is a two-state quantum degree of freedom

An electron spin state can be expressed in the \(z\)-measurement basis as a normalized two-component state:

\[ |\psi\rangle=\cos\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|\uparrow_z\rangle+e^{i\phi}\sin\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|\downarrow_z\rangle. \]
Spin is not literally a tiny charged ball rotating in ordinary space. It is intrinsic angular momentum with quantum transformation and measurement rules. The Bloch sphere represents a two-state space, not the electron’s physical surface.
Professor mode · chemistry connection

Many-electron spin adaptation, restricted/open-shell references, spin contamination, exchange, intersystem crossing, and spin–orbit coupling all build on this degree of freedom. A single spinor’s phase conventions must not be confused with observable many-electron spin populations.

Level 10 · Bridge to quantum chemistry

Electrons are indistinguishable fermions, so the many-electron state must be antisymmetric

For two electron labels, exchanging all spatial and spin coordinates reverses the wavefunction sign:

\[ \Psi(1,2)=-\Psi(2,1). \]

A Slater determinant enforces this rule automatically. The Pauli exclusion principle follows because a determinant vanishes if two columns—or two occupied spin orbitals—are identical.

\[ \Psi(x_1,\ldots,x_N)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \det\!\left[\chi_p(x_q)\right]. \]
1

One electron

States, probability, phase, operators, and solvable potentials establish the language.

N

Many electrons

Antisymmetry, electron repulsion, spin, and combinatorial state spaces create the electronic-structure problem.

≈

Methods

Basis sets, Hartree–Fock, DFT, correlation, multireference, and excited-state theories are controlled approximations to that problem.

Final rule: never judge a quantum-chemistry method by a single attractive orbital picture or energy. Ask what state, Hamiltonian, basis, approximation, observable, convention, and validation evidence produced the result.
