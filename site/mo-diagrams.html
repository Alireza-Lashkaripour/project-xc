MO diagrams for active-space intuition

Draw MO diagrams that reveal the chemistry, not just lines.

A useful MO diagram is a symmetry-, overlap-, and energy-matching argument. This guide turns that argument into a clean active-space checklist for occupied, virtual, lone-pair, π, σ, Rydberg, charge-transfer, and metal-centered orbitals.

Core rule. There is no single universal hand-drawn MO diagram for “any molecule.” The reliable workflow is: choose a geometry and fragments, classify fragment orbitals by symmetry, mix only same-symmetry orbitals with meaningful overlap and comparable energy, fill electrons for the target spin/state, and then verify the frontier shapes with computed orbitals or natural orbitals.

The 10-step construction algorithm

  1. Fix the structure and question. MO order and shapes depend on geometry, spin, charge, conformation, and whether you target ground states, bond breaking, excited states, redox, or reaction coordinates.
  2. Choose fragments. For diatomics the fragments are atoms. For polyatomics use chemically meaningful fragments: central atom + ligand SALCs, π system + substituents, donor + acceptor, metal + ligand field, or breaking bond pair.
  3. Choose axes and symmetry. Label orbitals as σ/π/δ for linear fragments, g/u for centrosymmetric molecules, and irreducible representations for point groups. Only orbitals of the same symmetry can interact.
  4. Estimate atomic/fragment orbital energies. Valence AOs from more electronegative atoms are lower in energy. Compact core-like orbitals are usually too low for frontier chemistry; diffuse Rydberg/continuum-like orbitals are high unless excited states demand them.
  5. Estimate overlap. Strong head-on overlap gives σ splitting; side-on overlap gives π splitting; four-lobed d interactions can give δ splitting. Orthogonal or spatially separated orbitals remain nonbonding or weakly coupled.
  6. Mix by the interaction rule. Same symmetry + good overlap + small energy gap gives strong bonding/antibonding splitting. Same symmetry orbitals repel: the lower one goes lower, the upper one goes higher.
  7. Track nodes and phases. Bonding combinations have constructive overlap between fragments; antibonding combinations have a node between fragments. More nodes usually means higher energy within the same symmetry family.
  8. Place nonbonding orbitals honestly. Lone pairs, metal d orbitals, radical centers, and symmetry-forbidden SALCs often sit between bonding and antibonding levels and frequently become frontier orbitals.
  9. Fill electrons for the actual state. Apply Pauli and Hund rules, but also respect open-shell coupling, near degeneracy, ligand-field splittings, and state symmetry.
  10. Validate against computation. Visualize canonical orbitals, localized orbitals, natural orbitals, and occupations. If the hand diagram and computed frontier shapes disagree, debug symmetry/geometry/localization before selecting the active space.

Minimal interaction picture

Every textbook MO diagram is built from repeated two-orbital interactions. The splitting grows when the fragment-orbital energy gap is small and the overlap/coupling is large.

What determines frontier-orbital shape?

Symmetry

Orbitals with different irreducible representations do not mix. Degenerate partners must be kept together when they are chemically active.

Energy match

Close fragment-orbital energies mix strongly. Large energy mismatch produces polarized bonding and weakly interacting nonbonding orbitals.

Overlap

Large spatial overlap makes large splitting. Poor overlap can leave a formally “available” orbital nearly nonbonding.

Nodes

Within a related set, more internal nodes and antibonding contacts usually mean higher energy. Nodal patterns help identify π*, σ*, and d-antibonding orbitals.

Electron count

Electron filling decides which levels are frontier. Open-shell species often require singly occupied orbitals and their coupling partners.

Geometry

Linear, bent, planar, and twisted geometries change symmetry and overlap. A MO diagram for one geometry may be wrong along a reaction path.

From MO diagram to active space

A good active space contains the orbitals whose occupations must be allowed to change. The hand-drawn diagram is a prefilter; final selection should be checked by occupations, orbital visualization, and state character.

Include pairs, not isolated fragments

If a bonding orbital is active because a bond can break or excite, include its antibonding partner. If π is active, include the relevant π* set. If a lone pair donates, include the acceptor.

Keep symmetry partners together

Degenerate π pairs, e/t₂ sets, and equivalent bonds should not be split unless the geometry or target state truly breaks that symmetry.

Balance across states

For excited states, include donor and acceptor orbitals for every important configuration: n→π*, π→π*, MLCT, LMCT, d–d, Rydberg, or bond-breaking channels.

Watch natural occupations

Closed orbitals near 2.00 and empty orbitals near 0.00 are usually inactive; orbitals with significant deviations, strong entanglement, or unstable occupations are active-space candidates.

Use automation as evidence

AVAS, UNO, entanglement/DMRG, and autoCAS-style workflows can suggest active orbitals, but the chemist must still check shapes, symmetry, and target-state balance.

Do not trust HOMO/LUMO blindly

Canonical orbitals can rotate within near-degenerate spaces, mix core/valence character, or reorder by method/basis. The active space is about chemically relevant occupation changes, not just orbital energy rank.

Practical active-space checklist

  1. State the target: ground-state correlation, bond breaking, conical intersection, spectrum, redox, spin crossover, or reaction barrier.
  2. Draw the minimal fragment/MO diagram and mark the orbitals whose occupation changes in the target process.
  3. Include complete bonding/antibonding, π/π*, lone-pair/acceptor, radical/SOMO, or metal d/ligand donor-acceptor sets as needed.
  4. Run a cheap calculation and visualize orbitals; relabel the diagram by actual shape, not by energy order alone.
  5. Inspect natural occupations from a correlated or preliminary multireference calculation. Flag orbitals far from 2 or 0, and near-degenerate partners.
  6. Check localization/orbital rotations: canonical MOs are not unique inside nearly degenerate subspaces; use localized or natural orbitals to see the chemistry.
  7. Verify all target states are described by the same active orbital window; avoid a space that is excellent for one state and biased against another.
  8. Document exclusions explicitly: core orbitals, very high Rydberg orbitals, σ frameworks, or inactive lone pairs should be excluded for a stated reason.

Common failure modes

  • HOMO/LUMO-only active spaces: often miss lower bonding partners, lone pairs, degenerate companions, or state-specific acceptors.
  • Unbalanced bond breaking: including σ* without σ, or one bond of an equivalent set, creates biased dissociation.
  • Missing lone pairs: n orbitals can dominate heteroatom photochemistry, protonation, and charge transfer.
  • Ignoring s-p or d-p mixing: same-symmetry orbital repulsion can reorder levels relative to a naive AO diagram.
  • Confusing canonical and chemical orbitals: canonical orbitals may delocalize over many fragments; localized/natural orbitals often reveal the active chemistry.
  • Using a diagram for the wrong geometry: twisting, bending, stretching, or symmetry lowering changes overlap and degeneracy.
