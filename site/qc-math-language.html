Level 1 · Complex amplitudes

A complex number stores magnitude and phase in one scalar

Quantum amplitudes are generally complex. Cartesian form separates real and imaginary components; polar form separates magnitude and phase:

\[ \begin{aligned} z&=x+iy=re^{i\phi},\\ &=r(\cos\phi+i\sin\phi),\\ z^*&=x-iy,\\ |z|^2&=z^*z=x^2+y^2. \end{aligned} \]
Re

Real component

The horizontal coordinate in the complex plane; it is not automatically an observable.

Im

Imaginary component

The coefficient of \(i\), where \(i^2=-1\); “imaginary” does not mean fictitious.

φ

Phase

An angle defined modulo \(2\pi\). Relative phase changes interference, while one global phase does not.

Misconception warning: complex phase is not a hidden spatial direction or a second probability. Probabilities come from squared magnitudes; phase controls how amplitudes combine.
Professor mode · fields and anti-linearity

A complex vector space allows complex scalar multiplication. Its inner product must conjugate one argument; without conjugation, a nonzero vector could fail to have a positive norm. Project XC uses the physics convention: conjugate-linear in the bra and linear in the ket.

Level 2 · State coordinates

A vector is the object; its coordinate list depends on the basis

Choose an orthonormal basis \(\{|e_i\rangle\}\). Then a state can be represented by coefficients \(c_i\), but the abstract state is not identical to any one list of coefficients:

\[ \begin{aligned} |\psi\rangle&=\sum_i c_i|e_i\rangle,\\ c_i&=\langle e_i|\psi\rangle,\\ \|\psi\|&=\sqrt{\langle\psi|\psi\rangle}. \end{aligned} \]

Coordinate list ≠ physical vector: changing an orthonormal basis changes the coefficients while preserving the represented state, norm, and basis-independent predictions.

Level 3 · Interactive lab 1

Projection separates the component you can represent from the residual you cannot

For a normalized direction \(|u\rangle\), the rank-one projector is \(P_u=|u\rangle\langle u|\). It splits any vector into parallel and orthogonal pieces:

\[ \begin{aligned} |v\rangle&=P_u|v\rangle\\ &\quad +(I-P_u)|v\rangle,\\ P_u|v\rangle&=|u\rangle\langle u|v\rangle,\\ \langle u|(I-P_u)|v\rangle&=0. \end{aligned} \]
Derivation path · why the residual is orthogonal

Let \(|r\rangle=|v\rangle-|u\rangle\langle u|v\rangle\). Then \(\langle u|r\rangle=\langle u|v\rangle-\langle u|u\rangle\langle u|v\rangle=0\) because \(\langle u|u\rangle=1\). Therefore \(\|v\|^2=|\langle u|v\rangle|^2+\|r\|^2\).

Level 4 · Interactive lab 2

A basis rotation changes coordinates without changing the vector

Let the columns of \(R(\theta)\) be a new orthonormal basis written in the old coordinates. A passive basis change uses \(R^T\) to obtain the new coordinates and \(R\) to reconstruct the original vector:

\[ \begin{aligned} R(\theta)&=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{pmatrix},\\ R^TR&=I,\\ c'&=R^Tc,\\ c&=Rc'. \end{aligned} \]
Convention warning: an active rotation of the vector and a passive rotation of its coordinate basis use closely related matrices but are not the same statement. Name what is held fixed before writing the transformation.
Professor mode · orbital rotations

For complex orthonormal bases, replace \(R^T\) by \(U^\dagger\). Occupied–occupied or virtual–virtual orbital rotations can change individual orbital pictures while leaving a single-determinant subspace or invariant energy unchanged; occupied–virtual rotations generally change the determinant.

Level 5 · Linear maps

A matrix is an operator representation after a basis has been chosen

For an orthonormal basis, \(|\psi\rangle=\sum_j c_j|e_j\rangle\) and \(A_{ij}=\langle e_i|\hat A|e_j\rangle\). If \(|\eta\rangle=\hat A|\psi\rangle\), ordinary matrix multiplication gives the output coefficients \(d_i\):

\[ \begin{aligned} d_i&=\sum_j A_{ij}c_j,\\ |\eta\rangle&=\hat A|\psi\rangle,\\ [AB]_{ij}&=\sum_k A_{ik}B_{kj}. \end{aligned} \]

S

Overlap matrix

In a nonorthogonal atomic-orbital basis, \(S_{\mu\nu}=\langle\chi_\mu|\chi_\nu\rangle\) and the metric is not the identity.

F

Fock/Kohn–Sham matrix

A basis representation of an effective one-electron operator that depends on the current density or orbitals.

P

Density matrix

Encodes one-particle occupancy information in a chosen basis; its elements are not individually basis invariant.

Matrix ≠ operator: the same operator has different matrices in different bases. Conversely, writing down numbers without defining the basis, ordering, metric, and units does not define a physical operator.

Level 6 · Interactive lab 3 and diagnostic game

An eigenvector is a direction a linear map does not turn

For \(Av=\lambda v\), the map may stretch, shrink, or reverse the direction but cannot rotate it away from itself. For any normalized trial direction, the residual diagnoses the failure:

\[ \begin{aligned} \lambda(v)&=v^TAv,\\ r(v)&=Av-\lambda(v)v,\\ \|r\|=0&\Longleftrightarrow v\text{ is an eigenvector}. \end{aligned} \]
Professor mode · degeneracy and generalized eigenproblems

A degenerate eigenspace does not select unique eigenvectors: any orthonormal rotation inside it remains valid. Atomic-orbital calculations commonly solve \(FC=SC\varepsilon\), a generalized Hermitian eigenproblem with overlap metric \(S\), rather than the standard \(Av=\lambda v\) game shown here.

Level 7 · Structure-preserving maps

Hermitian operators give real spectra; unitary maps preserve inner products

Hermitian\(A^\dagger=A\)Finite-dimensional eigenvalues are real; eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.
Unitary\(U^\dagger U=UU^\dagger=I\)Norms and inner products are preserved.
Projector\(P^2=P=P^\dagger\)An orthogonal projector keeps one subspace and removes its orthogonal complement.
Commutator\([A,B]=AB-BA\)Commuting Hermitian matrices can be simultaneously diagonalized under the finite-dimensional conditions used here.
\[ \begin{aligned} \langle Uu|Uv\rangle&=\langle u|U^\dagger U|v\rangle=\langle u|v\rangle,\\ \langle v|A|v\rangle^*&=\langle v|A^\dagger|v\rangle=\langle v|A|v\rangle. \end{aligned} \]
Vocabulary warning: Hermitian does not mean “all entries are real,” and unitary does not mean “each entry has magnitude one.” A complex Hermitian matrix may contain conjugate off-diagonal pairs; unitarity constrains complete rows and columns.
Professor mode · domains matter

For finite matrices, Hermitian and self-adjoint coincide in ordinary practice. Unbounded operators in infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces require a specified domain; symmetry of a formal differential expression alone is not enough to establish self-adjointness or a complete spectral resolution.

Level 8 · Composite spaces

Tensor products combine degrees of freedom—and create nonfactorizable states

If \(\mathcal H_A\) has basis \(|i\rangle\) and \(\mathcal H_B\) has basis \(|j\rangle\), the product space has basis \(|i\rangle\otimes|j\rangle\) and multiplied dimension:

\[ \begin{aligned} \dim(\mathcal H_A\otimes\mathcal H_B)&=\dim(\mathcal H_A)\\ &\quad\times\dim(\mathcal H_B),\\ |\Psi\rangle&=\sum_{ij}C_{ij}|i\rangle\otimes|j\rangle. \end{aligned} \]
2×2

Two two-state spaces

The product basis has four members: \(|00\rangle,|01\rangle,|10\rangle,|11\rangle\).

C

Coefficient array

If \(C_{ij}=a_i b_j\), the state factors. A general coefficient array need not have rank one.

N

Many orbitals

Product-space growth explains why exact many-electron representations become combinatorially expensive.

Tensor product ≠ antisymmetry: combining one-electron spaces creates the labeled product space. Fermionic antisymmetry is an additional physical constraint imposed by antisymmetrized products, determinants, or Fock-space operators.
Professor mode · entanglement and orbital representations

A state is entangled relative to a chosen subsystem factorization when it cannot be written as one product vector. This notion should not be casually equated with “electron correlation”: correlation measures and orbital partitions depend on the state, observable, and decomposition being used.

Level 9 · Interactive lab 4

Fourier transformation trades localization in one representation for width in its partner

This chapter uses a symmetric angular-wave-number convention. Naming the convention is essential because software and textbooks distribute factors of \(2\pi\) differently:

\[ \begin{aligned} \phi(k)&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{-ikx}\psi(x)\,dx,\\ \psi(x)&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{ikx}\phi(k)\,dk. \end{aligned} \]
Convention warning: \(k\) is angular wave number. Momentum is \(p=\hbar k\), so \(\sigma_x\sigma_k=1/2\) becomes \(\sigma_x\sigma_p=\hbar/2\). Using cycles per length would move factors of \(2\pi\).
Professor mode · what the Gaussian proves

The displayed Gaussian exactly saturates the transform-width bound under the stated convention. It illustrates the mechanism but does not imply every state is Gaussian or minimum uncertainty. Finite grids add sampling, truncation, aliasing, and discrete-transform normalization choices.

Level 10 · Variational bridge and chapter boss

A functional maps a whole trial object to a number

In an orthonormal coefficient representation, the Rayleigh quotient assigns a scalar to every nonzero coefficient vector. Requiring stationarity under admissible coefficient variations leads to an ordinary eigenvalue equation:

\[ \begin{aligned} R[c]&=\frac{c^\dagger Hc}{c^\dagger c},\\ \mathcal L[c]&=c^\dagger Hc-\lambda(c^\dagger c-1),\\ \delta\mathcal L[c]&=0,\\ &\Longrightarrow Hc=\lambda c. \end{aligned} \]

For a nonorthogonal basis with positive-definite overlap matrix \(S\), normalization and stationarity use the metric explicitly:

\[ \begin{aligned} R_S[c]&=\frac{c^\dagger Hc}{c^\dagger Sc},\\ c^\dagger Sc&=1,\\ &\Longrightarrow Hc=\lambda Sc. \end{aligned} \]
Derivation path · vary bra and ket consistently

For the finite orthonormal case, treat the complex coefficients and their conjugates as independent variation variables. Variation with respect to \(c^*\) gives \(Hc-\lambda c=0\); with a fixed nonorthogonal metric it instead gives \(Hc-\lambda Sc=0\). Variation with respect to \(c\) gives the conjugate equation. The lowest admissible Rayleigh quotient is the lowest eigenvalue. Higher eigenvectors are stationary points too, so “stationary” does not automatically mean “minimum.”
